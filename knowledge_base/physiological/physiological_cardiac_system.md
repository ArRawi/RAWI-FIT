# Cardiac System Science: Heart Function, Recovery & Performance Metrics

**Last updated:** 2026  
**Sources:** ACSM, ACCF, Journal of Cardiovascular Medicine, peer-reviewed research  
**Relevant for:** All training goals, recovery monitoring, cardiovascular health

---

## 1. Cardiac Output & Function

### 1.1 Cardiac Output Formula

**Cardiac Output (Q)** = Heart Rate (HR) × Stroke Volume (SV)

**In liters per minute:**
- Resting: 5 L/min (70 bpm × 71 ml)
- Maximum exercise: 25-40 L/min (depending on fitness)

**Implication:** Increasing either HR or SV improves oxygen delivery to muscles.

### 1.2 Stroke Volume Adaptations

**Training adaptations improve stroke volume:**

- **Increased ventricular volume** (heart chambers enlarge)
- **Improved contractility** (heart contracts more forcefully)
- **Enhanced diastolic filling** (better blood return to heart)

**Result:** Same heart rate, more oxygen delivery = greater aerobic capacity.

## 2. Heart Rate Response to Training

### 2.1 Maximum Heart Rate (MHR)

**Tanaka Formula (most accurate):**
```
MHR = 208 - (0.7 × age)
```

**For a 35-year-old:**
MHR = 208 - (0.7 × 35) = 208 - 24.5 = 183.5 bpm

**Note:** MHR decreases ~1 bpm per year with age; cannot be improved by training (genetic ceiling).

### 2.2 Resting Heart Rate (RHR)

**Definition:** Heart rate measured after ≥5 minutes rest, before getting out of bed.

**Typical values:**
- Sedentary: 60-100 bpm
- Trained endurance: 40-60 bpm
- Elite: 35-45 bpm

**Improves via:** Aerobic training (increased stroke volume allows lower HR at rest)

**Use as recovery indicator:**
- Normal: Consistent RHR day-to-day
- Elevated (5+ bpm above baseline): Indicates incomplete recovery or illness
- Action: If RHR elevated, reduce training volume or rest

### 2.3 Heart Rate Reserve (HRR)

**Formula:**
```
HRR = MHR - RHR
```

**For a 35-year-old with MHR=184 and RHR=50:**
HRR = 184 - 50 = 134 bpm

**Use:** Calculating heart rate training zones using Karvonen formula:

```
Target HR = (HRR × Intensity %) + RHR
```

**Example - Zone 2 (70% intensity):**
Target HR = (134 × 0.70) + 50 = 93.8 + 50 = 143.8 bpm

## 3. Heart Rate Variability (HRV)

### 3.1 What is HRV?

**Definition:** Variation in time intervals between heartbeats (measured in milliseconds).

**Healthy hearts show variation:**
- Beat 1→2: 850 ms
- Beat 2→3: 920 ms
- Beat 3→4: 880 ms

(Variation of 70 ms is normal; consistent 850-850-850 is abnormal)

### 3.2 HRV as Recovery Indicator

**High HRV (good):** Indicates parasympathetic dominance (recovery mode)
- Parasympathetic tone = rest and digest
- Allows adaptation to training stress
- Correlates with: good sleep, low stress, adequate nutrition

**Low HRV (concerning):** Indicates sympathetic dominance (stress mode)
- Sympathetic tone = fight or flight
- Impairs recovery; increases injury risk
- Correlates with: poor sleep, high stress, overtraining, illness

### 3.3 Practical HRV Monitoring

**Measurement:** Most phone apps or wearables provide HRV score (0-100).

**Interpretation:**
- Your baseline HRV (calculate 7-day average)
- If daily HRV drops 10%+ below baseline: recovery incomplete; reduce volume or rest
- If daily HRV elevated: good recovery; can handle higher intensity

**Example:**
- 7-day average HRV: 55
- Today's HRV: 48 (12% below baseline)
- Action: Rest day or low-intensity session; don't do hard workout

## 4. Cardiovascular Health Markers

### 4.1 Blood Pressure

**Normal:** <120/80 mmHg (systolic/diastolic)

**Elevation with training:** Temporary increase during/immediately post-exercise is normal.

**Resting BP:**
- <120/80: Normal
- 120-139 / 80-89: Elevated (monitor, lifestyle change)
- ≥140/90: High blood pressure (consult physician)

**Improvements via training:** Regular aerobic training reduces resting BP by 5-10 mmHg (ACSM).

### 4.2 Cholesterol Profile

**Training adaptations:**
- Increases HDL (good cholesterol): +5-10% with aerobic training
- Decreases LDL (bad cholesterol): -5-15% with aerobic training + proper nutrition
- Decreases triglycerides: -15-20% with consistent aerobic training

**Timeline:** 8-12 weeks for measurable changes.

### 4.3 Resting Metabolic Rate (RMR)

**Definition:** Calories burned at rest.

**Training effect:** 3-5% increase per 0.5 kg muscle gained (primarily from resistance training).

**Endurance training effect:** Minimal direct RMR increase; primarily improves fat oxidation capacity.

## 5. Cardiac Drift & Heat Stress

### 5.1 Cardiovascular Drift

**Definition:** During prolonged exercise, heart rate rises even though intensity constant.

**Mechanism:**
- Dehydration reduces blood volume
- Thermoregulation diverts blood to skin for cooling
- Heart rate compensates to maintain cardiac output

**Example:**
- Mile 1: 155 bpm at 9:00/mile pace
- Mile 5: 165 bpm at same 9:00/mile pace
- 10 bpm increase = cardiovascular drift

**Mitigation:**
- Hydrate adequately (0.4-0.8 L/hour depending on intensity)
- Consume carbohydrates (30-60g/hour for efforts >90 min)
- Acclimatize to heat (trains cardiovascular system to handle heat stress)

### 5.2 Heat Acclimation

**Adaptation:** 10-14 days of exercise in heat improves:
- Blood plasma volume (+5-10%)
- Sweat rate efficiency
- Skin blood flow regulation
- Temperature regulation

**Result:** Lower heart rate at same intensity when acclimated to heat.

## 6. Recovery & Cardiovascular Parasympathetic Response

### 6.1 Heart Rate Recovery (HRR₂₀)

**Definition:** How quickly heart rate drops after exercise stops.

**Measurement:** Heart rate 2 minutes after stopping intense exercise.

**Example:**
- Max HR during sprint workout: 185 bpm
- HR 2 minutes post-exercise: 125 bpm
- HRR₂₀ = 185 - 125 = 60 bpm drop

**Interpretation:**
- Rapid recovery (60+ bpm drop): Good parasympathetic tone; healthy recovery
- Slow recovery (30-40 bpm drop): Reduced parasympathetic activation; possible overtraining

**Training adaptation:** Aerobic training improves HRR₂₀ (faster recovery = better cardiac fitness).

### 6.2 Parasympathetic Activation

**Recovery requires parasympathetic nervous system activation.**

**Stimulation:**
- Deep breathing (5-10 minutes, 5-6 breaths/minute)
- Cool-down period (10-15 minutes easy recovery)
- Cold water immersion (2-3 minutes, 15°C water)
- Yoga/stretching
- Adequate sleep

**Suppression (avoid):**
- High stress
- Caffeine immediately post-workout
- Intense activity <2 hours post-workout
- Poor sleep

## 7. Cardiovascular Risk Assessment

### 7.1 ACSM Pre-Participation Screening

**Red flags requiring physician clearance:**
- Heart disease history
- Resting BP ≥160/100 mmHg
- High cholesterol (LDL >160 or total >240)
- Diabetes
- Age 45+ (men) or 55+ (women) planning vigorous exercise
- Symptoms: chest pain, shortness of breath, dizziness

**Action:** Consult physician before beginning intensive training if any flags present.

## 8. Feedback Cues for Reviews

✅ **Good signs:**
- Resting HR stable day-to-day (within ±5 bpm)
- Heart rate recovery normal for fitness level (60+ bpm drop in 2 min for trained)
- HRV stable or improving (within ±10% of baseline)
- Training intensity appropriate for fitness (not excessive HR for pace)
- Recovery days present (allows parasympathetic activation)
- Hydration adequate (no excessive HR drift during long efforts)

⚠️ **Areas to improve:**
- Resting HR elevated 5+ bpm above baseline (inadequate recovery; reduce volume)
- HRV declining trend (stress/overtraining; monitor closely)
- Excessive HR for given intensity (dehydration or overtraining)
- No recovery days (CNS/cardiac system always in sympathetic mode)
- HR drift >15 bpm during endurance efforts (dehydration; drink more)
- Rapid fatigue despite moderate intensity (possible overtraining syndrome; rest needed)
