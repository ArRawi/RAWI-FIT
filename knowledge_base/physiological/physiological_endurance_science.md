# Endurance Science: Aerobic Capacity, Energy Systems & VO2 Max

**Last updated:** 2026  
**Sources:** ACSM, ISSN, Journal of Applied Physiology, peer-reviewed research  
**Relevant for:** Running, cycling, swimming, cardio training, endurance goals

---

## 1. Energy Systems

### 1.1 Three Energy Systems

Human movement relies on three overlapping energy systems:

**1. Phosphocreatine (ATP-PC) System**
- Duration: 0-10 seconds
- Activity: All-out sprints
- Recovery: 3 minutes full recovery

**2. Anaerobic Glycolytic System**
- Duration: 10 seconds - 3 minutes
- Activity: High-intensity intervals, 400m sprint
- Recovery: 24-48 hours (lactate clearance, glycogen resynthesis)

**3. Aerobic Oxidative System**
- Duration: 3+ minutes
- Activity: Endurance training, Zone 2 cardio
- Recovery: Minimal fatigue (sustainable indefinitely)

**Endurance training primarily targets the aerobic system.**

### 1.2 Aerobic Capacity Components

**VO2 Max:** Maximum milliliters of oxygen per kilogram of bodyweight per minute (ml/kg/min).

**Formula:** VO2 = (Cardiac Output) × (Arterio-Venous Oxygen Difference)

Where:
- **Cardiac Output** = Heart Rate × Stroke Volume
- **A-V O2 Difference** = How efficiently muscles extract oxygen from blood

**Three ways to improve VO2 max:**
1. Increase heart rate capacity (less effective for endurance athletes)
2. Increase stroke volume (heart can pump more blood per beat)
3. Increase muscular oxygen extraction (mitochondrial density)

**Research (Seiler et al., 2011):** Aerobic endurance training primarily improves stroke volume and mitochondrial density, not maximum heart rate.

## 2. Heart Rate Zones & Training Intensities

### 2.1 Five Heart Rate Zones (Karvonen Formula)

**Zone 1 (Recovery):** 50-60% max heart rate
- Conversation pace; very easy
- Used: active recovery, easy base-building
- Duration: unlimited

**Zone 2 (Aerobic Base):** 60-70% max heart rate
- Comfortable; steady pace; breathing controlled
- Used: 80% of training volume for endurance athletes
- Builds fat oxidation, mitochondrial density, capillary density
- Duration: 30-120 minutes

**Zone 3 (Tempo/Threshold):** 70-80% max heart rate
- Moderate; conversation difficult; breathing elevated
- Used: 10-15% of training volume (1-2 sessions/week)
- Improves lactate threshold
- Duration: 20-45 minutes

**Zone 4 (Lactate Threshold/VO2 Max):** 80-90% max heart rate
- Hard; breathing difficult; can only speak single words
- Used: 5-10% of training volume (1 session/week max)
- Improves VO2 max and lactate clearance
- Duration: 20-40 minutes total (intervals)

**Zone 5 (Maximum/Anaerobic):** 90-100% max heart rate
- All-out; anaerobic; unsustainable
- Used: <5% training volume (rarely for endurance athletes)
- Risk of overtraining; limited aerobic benefit
- Duration: 1-10 minutes (short repeats)

### 2.2 The 80/20 Rule (Polarized Training)

**Research consensus (Seiler & Kemi, 2011):**
> Elite endurance athletes use approximately 80% of training time in Zone 1-2 (easy), and 20% in Zones 4-5 (hard). Almost no training in Zone 3.

**Why?** 
- Zone 2 builds aerobic capacity with minimal fatigue
- Zone 3 causes fatigue without sufficient stimulus for VO2 max
- Best results: most volume easy, infrequent hard intervals

**Common mistake:** Many endurance athletes train Zone 3 too much ("moderate pace"), depleting glycogen and impairing recovery without sufficient gains.

## 3. Aerobic Adaptation Mechanisms

### 3.1 Mitochondrial Density

**Definition:** Number of mitochondria per muscle fiber.

**Impact:** Higher mitochondrial density = better oxygen utilization = greater aerobic capacity.

**Training stimulus:**
- Zone 2 training (60-70% max HR) for 45-120 minutes
- Sufficient duration to trigger mitochondrial biogenesis
- Consistency: 3-4 Zone 2 sessions per week

**Improvement timeline:** 4-8 weeks for measurable increase; 12+ weeks for significant adaptation.

### 3.2 Capillary Density

**Definition:** Number of blood vessels per muscle fiber.

**Impact:** More capillaries = better oxygen delivery to muscles.

**Training stimulus:** Zone 2 aerobic training; endurance sports

**Improvement timeline:** 8-12 weeks

### 3.3 Stroke Volume

**Definition:** Amount of blood heart pumps per beat.

**Impact:** Higher stroke volume = more oxygen delivery at any heart rate.

**Training stimulus:**
- Zone 2 long-duration training (90-120 min)
- Zone 4 interval training (VO2 max work)
- Frequency: 2-3 sessions per week

**Improvement timeline:** 6-12 weeks

### 3.4 Lactate Threshold

**Definition:** Highest intensity you can sustain without lactate accumulating.

**Impact:** Higher threshold = can go faster while staying aerobic.

**Training stimulus:** Zone 3-4 threshold work (80-90% max HR)

**Typical session:** 2-3 × 8-10 minute efforts at Zone 4, 3-minute easy recovery between

## 4. VO2 Max Training

### 4.1 What is VO2 Max?

VO2 max is your body's maximum capacity to utilize oxygen during intense exercise.

**Average values (ACSM):**
- Untrained men: 35-40 ml/kg/min
- Untrained women: 27-30 ml/kg/min
- Endurance athletes (men): 60-85 ml/kg/min
- Endurance athletes (women): 50-75 ml/kg/min
- Elite distance runners (men): 70-85 ml/kg/min

**Can be improved by 15-25% with training.**

### 4.2 VO2 Max Training Intervals

**Research (Billat et al., 2000; Seiler, 2010):**
> Intervals at 90-100% VO2 max (Zone 5) for 3-8 minutes are most effective for raising VO2 max.

**Example VO2 max session:**
- Warm-up: 10 minutes easy (Zone 1-2)
- Main set: 4 × 4 minutes at Zone 5 (90%+ max HR), 3 minutes easy recovery
- Cool-down: 10 minutes easy

**Frequency:** 1x per week maximum (high fatigue; requires full recovery)

**Duration benefit:** 3-minute intervals are minimally effective; 5-8 minute intervals at VO2 max are optimal.

## 5. Training Periodisation for Endurance

### 5.1 Build Phase (8-12 weeks)

**Goal:** Build aerobic base before intensifying.

**Structure:**
- Weeks 1-3: Zone 1-2 only; gradual volume increase
- Weeks 4-6: One Zone 3 session added; rest Zone 2
- Weeks 7-9: One Zone 4 interval session; rest Zone 2
- Weeks 10-12: One Zone 5 session; rest Zone 2

**Example weekly structure (40 km running):**
- Mon: 8 km Zone 2
- Tue: 5 km Zone 1 recovery
- Wed: 10 km with 4 × 3 min Zone 4 (threshold work)
- Thu: 6 km Zone 1 recovery
- Fri: 12 km Zone 2 long run
- Sat: 5 km Zone 1
- Sun: Rest

### 5.2 Peak Phase (3-4 weeks)

**Goal:** Maximize VO2 max and lactate threshold.

**Structure:**
- One VO2 max session (Zone 5): 4-5 × 4-5 min intervals
- One threshold session (Zone 4): 2-3 × 8-10 min efforts
- One long run (Zone 2): 80-120 minutes
- 2-3 easy sessions (Zone 1-2)

**Total volume:** 80% Zone 1-2, 20% Zones 4-5 (minimal Zone 3)

### 5.3 Taper & Recovery (2 weeks)

**Goal:** Recover from training while maintaining fitness.

**Structure:**
- Reduce volume by 40-50%
- Maintain intensity (keep hard sessions but fewer repeats)
- More easy sessions
- Increase sleep

## 6. Metrics & Progression

### 6.1 Weekly Mileage (Running)

**Beginner:** 15-20 km/week

**Intermediate:** 30-50 km/week

**Advanced:** 50-100+ km/week

**10% Rule:** Increase weekly mileage by maximum 10% per week; increases beyond this elevate injury risk.

### 6.2 Long Run Progression

**Beginner:** 30-45 minutes Zone 2

**Intermediate:** 60-90 minutes Zone 2

**Advanced:** 90-120+ minutes Zone 2

**Rule:** Long run is 25-30% of weekly volume; done once per week with 5-7 days recovery before hard session.

### 6.3 Resting Heart Rate (RHR)

**Indicator of aerobic fitness & recovery.**

- Untrained: 60-80 bpm
- Trained endurance athletes: 40-50 bpm
- Elite: 35-45 bpm

**RHR elevated by:** Poor sleep, illness, overtraining, inadequate nutrition

**Track weekly:** If RHR elevated 5+ bpm above baseline, suggests incomplete recovery; reduce volume.

## 7. Common Mistakes

**Mistake 1: Training Too Hard Too Often**
- Problem: Zone 3 "moderate" pace most sessions; fatigue accumulates
- Fix: 80% Zone 1-2, 20% Zone 4-5; almost no Zone 3

**Mistake 2: Insufficient Long Run**
- Problem: All sessions moderate; no extended Zone 2 training
- Fix: One 90-120 minute Zone 2 session per week

**Mistake 3: Excessive Hard Sessions**
- Problem: 2-3 hard sessions per week; insufficient recovery
- Fix: 1 VO2 max + 1 threshold session per week maximum

**Mistake 4: No Adequate Rest**
- Problem: Running hard-easy-hard without recovery day
- Fix: At least 2-3 full rest days or Zone 1 recovery days per week

**Mistake 5: Ignoring the 10% Rule**
- Problem: Increasing mileage 20-30% per week; overuse injuries
- Fix: Maximum 10% increase; deload every 3-4 weeks

**Mistake 6: Inadequate Nutrition/Hydration**
- Problem: Under-fueling long efforts; glycogen depletion
- Fix: 30-60g carbs per hour for efforts >90 min; proper hydration

## 8. Feedback Cues for Reviews

✅ **Good signs:**
- 80%+ of training volume in Zone 1-2 (easy)
- One dedicated hard session (Zone 4-5) per week
- One long-duration session per week (90+ min Zone 2)
- Progressive volume increase (10% per week)
- Weekly mileage consistent (sustainable)
- Rest/recovery days present (2-3 per week)
- RHR stable or improving

⚠️ **Areas to improve:**
- Most sessions moderate intensity (Zone 3); insufficient easy/hard contrast
- Multiple hard sessions without recovery (overtraining risk)
- Long run absent or too short (<60 min)
- Excessive volume increase (>10% per week; injury risk)
- Insufficient rest days (training 7 days/week)
- RHR elevated (inadequate recovery)
- No periodisation visible (random intensities)
