## CATEGORY 1 — TRAINING FUNDAMENTALS

**1RM (One-Rep Max)**
The maximum weight a person can lift for exactly one repetition of an exercise with proper form. Used as a reference point for calculating training loads (e.g. "work at 75% of 1RM"). Can be tested directly or estimated using rep-max formulas (e.g. Epley: 1RM ≈ weight × (1 + reps/30)).

**AMRAP (As Many Reps / Rounds As Possible)**
A training format where you perform as many repetitions or rounds as possible within a set time, or until failure. Tests muscular endurance and work capacity.

**Compound Exercise**
A multi-joint movement that recruits multiple muscle groups simultaneously. Examples: squat, deadlift, bench press, pull-up, overhead press, row. Foundation of most strength programs — greater stimulus, better hormonal response, more time-efficient.

**Concentric**
The phase of a muscle contraction where the muscle shortens under load. The "lifting" phase. Example: raising the bar in a bicep curl. Concentric-focused training builds power and strength.

**Cool-Down**
5–10 minutes of low-intensity activity and/or static stretching performed after a workout. Gradually reduces heart rate, prevents blood pooling, aids in recovery and flexibility.

**Deload**
A planned reduction in training volume (typically 40–50%) and/or intensity for 1 week, performed every 4–8 weeks. Allows the body to recover fully before the next training block, dissipates accumulated fatigue, and often results in performance improvements in the following week.

**DOMS (Delayed Onset Muscle Soreness)**
Muscle soreness and stiffness that appears 24–72 hours after novel or intense exercise, caused by microscopic muscle fiber damage. Normal and part of the adaptation process. Not an injury. Should resolve within 3–5 days. Training through mild DOMS is generally safe; severe DOMS in the same muscle group suggests inadequate recovery.

**Drop Set**
An advanced technique where you perform a set to near-failure, immediately reduce the weight (typically 20–30%), then continue for additional reps with no rest. Increases metabolic stress and volume within a short time. Best used sparingly by intermediate+ lifters.

**Eccentric**
The phase where a muscle lengthens under load — the "lowering" phase. Example: lowering the bar in a bench press; descending in a squat. Eccentric loading causes more muscle damage than concentric and is a primary driver of hypertrophy and DOMS. Controlled eccentrics (3–4 seconds) increase time under tension.

**EMOM (Every Minute On the Minute)**
A training format where you perform a prescribed exercise(s) at the start of each minute, rest for the remainder, then repeat. Used for conditioning, skill practice, and metabolic training.

**Failure (Training to Failure)**
Reaching the point where another rep cannot be completed with proper form. Training to or near failure is effective for hypertrophy but should not be done on every set or with high-risk movements (deadlift, squat). Leaving 1–3 reps in reserve (RIR 1–3) is generally recommended for most working sets.

**Isolation Exercise**
A single-joint movement targeting one muscle group. Examples: bicep curl, leg extension, lateral raise. Used to supplement compound work, address weak links, or increase volume for a specific muscle.

**Isometric**
A muscle contraction with no joint movement — the muscle produces force but doesn't change length. Examples: plank, wall sit, L-sit hold. Builds strength at a specific joint angle and improves joint stability.

**Microcycle**
The smallest training unit — typically one week. Contains individual sessions planned around a weekly structure.

**Mesocycle**
A training block of 3–8 weeks with a specific training focus (e.g. hypertrophy block, strength block, deload). Mesocycles build on each other within a macrocycle.

**Macrocycle**
The overall training plan — typically 3–12 months. Encompasses multiple mesocycles planned to peak performance at a target date.

**Periodization**
The systematic organization of training into phases (macrocycles → mesocycles → microcycles) with varying volume, intensity, and exercise selection to prevent plateaus, manage fatigue, and peak performance at the right time. Major types:
- **Linear periodization:** Gradually increase intensity, decrease volume over weeks
- **Undulating periodization (DUP):** Vary rep ranges within the same week (e.g. heavy Mon, moderate Wed, light Fri)
- **Block periodization:** Distinct phases each emphasizing different qualities (e.g. hypertrophy → strength → power)

**Progressive Overload**
The fundamental principle of training adaptation — gradually increasing the training stimulus over time (via more weight, more reps, more sets, reduced rest, or harder exercise variations) to continue forcing the body to adapt. Without progressive overload, strength and muscle gains plateau.

**Rep (Repetition)**
One complete movement of an exercise from start position to end position and back. A bicep curl from arms extended to fully contracted and back = 1 rep.

**RIR (Reps in Reserve)**
The number of additional reps a person could have performed before reaching failure. RIR 0 = failure. RIR 1 = could do one more. RIR 2–3 = moderate effort. Used to auto-regulate training intensity based on daily readiness. More practical than always training to failure.

**RPE (Rate of Perceived Exertion)**
A subjective self-assessment of effort on a scale of 1–10 (or 6–20 Borg scale).

| RPE (1–10) | Description | Equivalent RIR |
|---|---|---|
| 1–3 | Very easy — warm-up level | Many reps left |
| 4–5 | Moderate — could sustain a long time | 6+ reps left |
| 6–7 | Hard — working but manageable | 3–4 reps left |
| 8 | Very hard | 2 reps left |
| 9 | Near max | 1 rep left |
| 10 | Maximum effort — absolute failure | 0 reps left |

**Set**
A group of consecutive repetitions performed without rest. "3 sets of 10 reps" = 10 reps, rest, 10 reps, rest, 10 reps.

**Superset**
Performing two exercises back-to-back with no rest between them, then resting after both are complete.
- **Antagonist superset:** Opposing muscle groups (e.g. bicep curl + tricep extension) — efficient; minimal performance loss
- **Agonist superset (compound set):** Same muscle group (e.g. bench press + chest fly) — high fatigue; intensity technique

**Tempo**
The speed at which each phase of a lift is performed, written as X-Y-Z (eccentric-pause-concentric) in seconds. Example: 3-1-1 = 3 seconds lowering, 1 second pause, 1 second lifting. Slower tempos increase time under tension and hypertrophy stimulus.

**Time Under Tension (TUT)**
Total time a muscle is under load during a set. A set of 10 reps at 3-1-1 tempo = ~50 seconds TUT. Higher TUT (40–70 sec per set) is associated with hypertrophy.

**Training Volume**
Total workload = sets × reps × weight. Also expressed as "weekly sets per muscle group" for planning purposes.

**Unilateral Training**
Training one limb at a time (e.g. single-leg squat, single-arm row). Essential for detecting and correcting left/right strength asymmetries, improving balance and coordination.

**Warm-Up**
Preparatory activity before main training. Includes: general warm-up (5–10 min light cardio), dynamic mobility drills, and movement-specific warm-up sets at lighter loads.

## CATEGORY 2 — CARDIOVASCULAR & ENERGY SYSTEMS

**Aerobic**
Energy production using oxygen. Sustains lower-to-moderate intensity activity for extended periods. Primary system for Zone 1–3 cardio. Fueled predominantly by fat (low intensity) and glycogen (moderate intensity).

**Anaerobic**
Energy production without oxygen. Used for short, high-intensity efforts (Zone 4–5, sprints, heavy lifting). Produces lactate as a byproduct. Can only be sustained for seconds to minutes.

**Cardiorespiratory Fitness (CRF)**
The ability of the heart, lungs, and blood vessels to deliver oxygen to working muscles during sustained exercise. Best measured by VO2 max. The single strongest predictor of longevity and chronic disease risk.

**Heart Rate Reserve (HRR)**
HRR = Maximum Heart Rate − Resting Heart Rate. Used in the Karvonen formula to calculate personalized heart rate training zones. Accounts for individual fitness level, unlike simple % MHR.

**HIIT (High-Intensity Interval Training)**
Alternating short bouts of near-maximal effort (Zone 4–5, 20 sec–4 min) with recovery periods (Zone 1–2). Effective for improving VO2 max and lactate threshold. Should be limited to 1–2 sessions per week for most people; higher frequency increases injury and overtraining risk.

**Lactate Threshold (LT)**
The exercise intensity at which lactate begins to accumulate faster than it can be cleared. Training around LT (Zone 4) improves the ability to sustain higher intensities for longer. Also called "anaerobic threshold" or "threshold pace."

**LISS (Low-Intensity Steady State)**
Sustained cardio at Zone 1–2 for an extended duration (30–90 min). Primary fat-burning zone; supports recovery; low injury risk. Examples: brisk walking, easy cycling.

**Maximum Heart Rate (MHR)**
The highest heart rate achievable during maximal exercise. Used to calculate training zones.
- Classic formula: `220 − age`
- Tanaka formula (more accurate): `208 − (0.7 × age)`

**MET (Metabolic Equivalent of Task)**
A measure of exercise intensity relative to rest. 1 MET = sitting at rest. Light activity = 1.5–3 METs. Moderate = 3–6 METs. Vigorous = ≥ 6 METs.

**Resting Heart Rate (RHR)**
Heart rate at complete rest, typically measured in the morning before getting up. Normal range: 60–100 bpm (AHA). Athletic range: 40–60 bpm. Lower = more efficient heart.

**VO2 Max**
Maximum volume of oxygen the body can use per minute per kilogram of body weight (ml/kg/min). The gold-standard measure of aerobic fitness. Declines ~10% per decade without training; can be improved 15–25% in a few months of aerobic training.

**Zone Training (Zones 1–5)**
Training intensity zones based on % of maximum heart rate or heart rate reserve. See `health_guidelines_reference.md` for full zone definitions and personalized calculations.

| Zone | Name | % MHR | Feel |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Recovery | 50–60% | Very easy; can hold full conversation |
| 2 | Aerobic Base | 60–70% | Easy; comfortable conversation |
| 3 | Moderate | 70–80% | Short sentences; working but manageable |
| 4 | Threshold | 80–90% | Few words; hard effort |
| 5 | Max Effort | 90–100% | Cannot speak; maximum output |

## CATEGORY 3 — MUSCLE PHYSIOLOGY

**Agonist**
The primary mover in an exercise — the muscle doing the main work. Example: biceps brachii in a bicep curl.

**Antagonist**
The opposing muscle to the agonist. Must relax to allow the agonist to contract. Example: triceps during a bicep curl. Training antagonist pairs evenly prevents imbalances and injury.

**Atrophy**
Muscle loss / decrease in muscle size, caused by disuse, inactivity, prolonged caloric deficit, or illness. Resistance training and adequate protein intake are the primary ways to prevent atrophy.

**Connective Tissue**
Tendons (muscle-to-bone) and ligaments (bone-to-bone). Adapt more slowly to training stress than muscles — a major reason not to progress weight faster than the 10% rule allows.

**Fast-Twitch Muscle Fibers (Type II)**
Recruited for high-intensity, explosive efforts. Generate force quickly but fatigue rapidly. Trained by heavy lifting (Type IIb) and power/speed work (Type IIa). Greater potential for hypertrophy than slow-twitch fibers.

**Hypertrophy**
The increase in muscle cell size (not number) due to training. Driven by three mechanisms: mechanical tension (load), metabolic stress (fatigue/pump), and muscle damage (DOMS). Optimal rep range: 6–20 reps with sufficient volume (10–20 sets/week).

**Motor Unit**
A motor neuron and all the muscle fibers it controls. Heavier loads and near-failure training recruit more motor units, producing greater strength and hypertrophy stimulus.

**Muscle Protein Synthesis (MPS)**
The process by which the body builds new muscle proteins in response to training and dietary protein. Stimulated by resistance training and amino acid availability. Peaks 0–3 hours post-exercise and remains elevated for up to 24 hours.

**Slow-Twitch Muscle Fibers (Type I)**
Recruited for sustained, lower-intensity efforts. Resistant to fatigue; high endurance capacity. Trained by higher-rep sets, aerobic conditioning. Less hypertrophy potential than fast-twitch.

**Muscle Protein Breakdown (MPB)**
The opposing process to MPS — the breakdown of muscle proteins. Net muscle gain requires MPS > MPB. Protein intake and resistance training shift the balance toward MPS.

## CATEGORY 4 — NUTRITION & BODY COMPOSITION

**BMR (Basal Metabolic Rate)**
Calories needed to sustain basic bodily functions at complete rest (breathing, circulation, digestion). Starting point for calculating total calorie needs.

**Caloric Deficit**
Consuming fewer calories than TDEE. Required for fat loss. Moderate deficit: 300–500 kcal/day. Too large a deficit risks muscle loss, hormonal disruption, and metabolic adaptation.

**Caloric Surplus**
Consuming more calories than TDEE. Required for muscle gain beyond beginner phase. Lean bulk: +200–300 kcal/day. Excess surplus adds fat without building muscle faster.

**Glycogen**
The stored form of carbohydrates in muscles and liver. Primary fuel for moderate-to-high intensity exercise. Depleted during hard training sessions; replenished by consuming carbohydrates post-workout and throughout the day.

**Macronutrients (Macros)**
The three major nutrients providing energy: Protein (4 kcal/g), Carbohydrates (4 kcal/g), Fat (9 kcal/g).

**NEAT (Non-Exercise Activity Thermogenesis)**
Calories burned through all non-exercise movement — walking to work, fidgeting, doing household chores. Highly variable between individuals (400–2000+ kcal/day difference). Major reason why two people with the same body weight and exercise routine can have very different caloric needs.

**Recomposition (Recomp)**
Simultaneously losing fat and gaining muscle, typically at or near maintenance calories. Most effective for beginners, deconditioned individuals, or those with higher body fat percentages.

**TDEE (Total Daily Energy Expenditure)**
Total calories burned in a day = BMR + exercise + NEAT + thermic effect of food. The correct baseline from which to calculate cutting and bulking calorie targets. Recalculate every 2–4 weeks or after significant weight change.

**Thermic Effect of Food (TEF)**
Calories burned digesting and processing food. Protein has the highest TEF (~25–30% of its calories). Accounts for ~10% of TDEE.

## CATEGORY 5 — PROGRAM DESIGN TERMS

**Frequency**
How often a muscle group or movement pattern is trained per week. Optimal frequency for most goals: 2–3x per muscle group per week (ACSM 2026).

**Intensity**
How heavy the load is, expressed as % of 1RM. Alternatively expressed as RPE or RIR. High intensity = heavier weight, lower reps (strength). Moderate intensity = moderate weight, moderate reps (hypertrophy).

**Progression**
The process of making training harder over time to continue driving adaptation. Can mean adding weight, reps, sets, reducing rest, or advancing to harder exercise variations. Required for continued strength and muscle gain beyond the beginner stage.

**Progressive Overload**
See Category 1. The core principle of all effective training programs.

**Split**
How training is divided across the week. Common splits:
- **Full Body:** All major muscle groups each session (3×/week)
- **Upper/Lower:** Upper body days + Lower body days (4×/week)
- **Push/Pull/Legs (PPL):** Separated by movement pattern (3–6×/week)
- **Body Part Split ("Bro Split"):** One major muscle group per day (5–6×/week)

**Training Age**
How many years a person has been consistently and intentionally resistance training. Key variable for selecting appropriate programs and setting realistic expectations.

**Volume**
Total workload. In resistance training: sets × reps × weight. Or simplified to "weekly sets per muscle group" for planning. In cardio: total time or distance per week.

## CATEGORY 6 — INJURY & RECOVERY TERMS

**Acute Injury**
Sudden-onset injury from a single traumatic event (e.g. muscle tear, sprain). Opposite of overuse injury.

**Active Recovery**
Low-intensity movement on rest days to promote blood flow and reduce soreness without adding training stress. Examples: Zone 1 walk, yoga, gentle swimming. Preferred over complete inactivity for most people.

**RICE Protocol (Rest, Ice, Compression, Elevation)**
Standard first response to acute soft tissue injuries (sprains, strains, bruising) within the first 48–72 hours. Modern guidelines (PRICE / POLICE) add: Protection, Optimal Loading (gradual return to movement).

**Overtraining Syndrome**
A serious condition caused by excessive training without adequate recovery over weeks to months. Symptoms: persistent performance decline, fatigue, illness, mood disturbances. Requires weeks to months of reduced training for recovery.

**Overuse Injury**
Gradual-onset injury from repetitive stress exceeding tissue repair capacity. Most common in resistance training. Prevention: follow the 10% rule; balance push:pull ratios; include rest days.

**Tendinitis / Tendinopathy**
Inflammation (tendinitis) or degeneration (tendinopathy) of a tendon from overuse or repetitive strain. Common sites: patellar tendon (below knee), Achilles tendon, rotator cuff tendons, elbow tendons. Treatment: load management, eccentric exercises, physiotherapy.

## CATEGORY 7 — MEASUREMENT & TESTING

**BMI (Body Mass Index)**
`BMI = weight_kg / height_m²`. A proxy measure for body fat using only height and weight. Does not account for muscle mass — a muscular person may have high BMI without excess fat. Used for population-level health categorization, not individual body composition assessment.

**Body Fat Percentage**
The proportion of total body weight that is fat mass. Measured by DEXA scan (most accurate), skinfold calipers, or bioelectrical impedance. More informative than BMI for body composition.

**HRV (Heart Rate Variability)**
Variation in time between consecutive heartbeats. Higher HRV generally indicates better recovery status, autonomic nervous system balance, and fitness. Tracked by many wearables. A declining HRV trend can signal inadequate recovery or overtraining.

**1RM Estimation Formula (Epley)**
`Estimated 1RM = weight × (1 + reps / 30)`
Most accurate for sets of 1–10 reps. Example: 80 kg × 8 reps → 1RM ≈ 80 × (1 + 8/30) = 80 × 1.267 = 101 kg.

**Relative Strength**
Lift performance expressed as a multiple of body weight. `Relative strength = weight_lifted / body_weight`. Allows fair comparison between individuals of different sizes.
