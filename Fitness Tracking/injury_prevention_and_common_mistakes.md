# Injury Prevention & Common Mistakes

## PART 1 — INJURY OVERVIEW

### Most Common Injury Sites in Resistance Training

Based on research across gym populations (PMC study, n=1012 gym participants):

| Injury Site | Prevalence | Primary Cause |
|---|---|---|
| **Shoulder** | ~25% of injuries | Improper overhead technique; push:pull imbalance; rotator cuff overuse |
| **Knee** | ~20% of injuries | Valgus collapse (knees caving); overloading; poor alignment |
| **Lower Back** | ~18% of injuries | Rounded spine during hinge movements; excessive load; core weakness |
| **Elbow / Wrist** | ~10% | Overuse; locking out joints; excessive curling volume |
| **Biceps / Pec Tear** | < 5% | Acute overload; no warm-up; excessive weight |

> "The most common injuries in resistance training are muscle strains from overuse or acute injury — the back, shoulder and knee being the most frequent sites." — Houston Methodist Sports Medicine

> Source: *Most Common Injuries in Resistance Training* (PMC, 2025); Houston Methodist; USA Weightlifting

---

### Injury Type Classification

| Type | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Acute** | Sudden onset during a single event | Pec tear on bench press; hamstring strain on deadlift |
| **Overuse** | Gradual onset from repetitive stress | Patellar tendinitis; shoulder impingement; tennis elbow |
| **DOMS** | Delayed onset muscle soreness | Normal 24–72h post-exercise soreness; NOT an injury |

## PART 2 — COMMON FORM ERRORS BY EXERCISE

### Squat

| Error | Risk | Correction |
|---|---|---|
| Knees caving inward (valgus collapse) | Meniscus tear, ACL strain, patellar tendinitis | Cue "knees over toes"; strengthen glutes; use lighter load |
| Lower back rounding (butt wink at depth) | Disc herniation, lumbar strain | Improve hip mobility; reduce depth to pain-free range; strengthen core |
| Forward lean / chest dropping | Lower back overload | Strengthen upper back; use goblet squat to practice upright torso |
| Heels rising | Ankle mobility deficit | Heel elevation trick (plates); work ankle mobility daily |
| Squatting too shallow (no depth) | Reduces glute/hamstring involvement; shifts to quads only | Hip crease below parallel for full ROM |

---

### Deadlift

| Error | Risk | Correction |
|---|---|---|
| Rounded lower back (lumbar flexion) | Disc herniation; acute lumbar strain | "Chest up, proud chest"; brace core before lift; reduce weight |
| Jerking bar off the floor | Acute muscle/ligament strain | Establish tension before pulling; "leg press the floor" cue |
| Bar drifting away from body | Greatly increased lower back lever arm | Keep bar scraping shins; use chalk/straps for grip |
| Hyperextending at lockout | Lower back strain | Stop at neutral standing; don't lean back at top |
| Looking up excessively | Cervical spine strain | Neutral neck — eyes forward/slightly down |

---

### Bench Press

| Error | Risk | Correction |
|---|---|---|
| Elbow flare (> 90° from torso) | Shoulder impingement; rotator cuff strain | Tuck elbows to 45–75°; widen grip slightly |
| Bouncing bar off chest | Sternum bruise; pec tear risk | Controlled touch; 1-sec pause at chest or slight touch |
| Excessive arch (bridge) | Lower back strain | Moderate arch acceptable; feet flat and braced |
| Wrists bending back | Wrist strain | Wrap wrists; grip bar lower in palm |
| Uneven pressing (one side higher) | Shoulder imbalance | Use dumbbells temporarily to correct imbalance |

---

### Overhead Press

| Error | Risk | Correction |
|---|---|---|
| Excessive lumbar arch (rib flare) | Lower back strain | Brace core; "squeeze glutes"; tuck ribs down |
| Bar path too far forward | Shoulder impingement | Keep bar over mid-foot; press straight up |
| Pressing too heavy before mobility allows | Shoulder impingement | Develop shoulder mobility before heavy loading |
| Locking neck forward | Cervical strain | "Make room for the bar" — head neutral; bar passes face |

---

### Barbell Row

| Error | Risk | Correction |
|---|---|---|
| Excessive body momentum / cheating | Lower back strain; reduces target muscle stimulus | Use controlled form; lighter load; strict reps |
| Rounding upper back | Thoracic spine stress | Retract scapulae before pulling; maintain flat back |
| Pulling to wrong point (stomach vs lower chest) | Changes muscle target | Pull to lower sternum/upper navel for mid-back |

---

### Pull-Up / Chin-Up

| Error | Risk | Correction |
|---|---|---|
| Kipping / swinging for momentum | Shoulder labrum strain; rotator cuff | Use strict form; build strength first with assisted pull-up |
| Not going through full ROM | Reduced lat development; can cause shoulder issues | Full hang at bottom; chin fully above bar at top |
| Shrugging shoulders at bottom | Shoulder impingement | Depress scapulae (shoulders down) before pulling |

---

### Lunge / Split Squat

| Error | Risk | Correction |
|---|---|---|
| Front knee collapsing inward | Knee ligament stress | Cue knee track over second toe; strengthen glutes |
| Torso leaning excessively forward | Hip flexor strain; knee stress | Keep torso upright; take longer step |
| Rear heel not grounded (standard lunge) | Balance and ankle issues | Work hip flexor mobility |

## PART 3 — INJURY PREVENTION PRINCIPLES

### The FITT-VP Principle (ACSM)

Before adding load or volume, ensure:
- **Frequency** — adequate rest between muscle groups (48h minimum)
- **Intensity** — don't exceed form capacity; leave 1–3 reps in reserve (RIR 1–3)
- **Time** — don't increase session volume by > 10% per week
- **Type** — include mobility, warm-up, and cool-down in every session
- **Volume** — weekly sets within guidelines; avoid sudden spikes
- **Progression** — gradual, not sudden

> Source: ACSM GETP 11th Ed.

---

### The 10% Rule

**Never increase total weekly training volume by more than 10% per week.**

This applies to:
- Total sets per muscle group per week
- Running / cardio mileage or time
- Weight on the bar

Rapid volume spikes are a primary cause of overuse injuries.

---

### Warm-Up Requirements

| Component | Prescription | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| General warm-up | 5–10 min light cardio (Zone 1) | Raise core temperature; increase blood flow |
| Dynamic mobility | 5 min — leg swings, arm circles, hip rotations | Increase joint ROM; activate movement patterns |
| Movement-specific warm-up | 1–2 warm-up sets at 50–70% working weight | Neural activation; practice movement pattern before loading |

**Never skip warm-up before heavy compound lifts (squat, deadlift, bench, overhead press).**

---

### Push:Pull Balance (Injury Prevention)

Shoulder impingement and rotator cuff injuries are strongly correlated with pushing > pulling:

| Recommendation | Source |
|---|---|
| Minimum 1:1 push:pull ratio | General sports science consensus |
| 1:2 push:pull (recommended) | Mission MVMT; strength & conditioning coaches |
| 1:3 push:pull (corrective for existing shoulder issues) | Rehabilitation-focused coaches |

**Key neglected muscles to always include:**
- Rear delts (face pulls, reverse flys)
- External rotators (band external rotations)
- Lower traps (Y-raises)
- Rhomboids (rows)

---

### Quad-Dominant vs Posterior Chain Balance

| Imbalance | Risk | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Strong quads, weak hamstrings/glutes | Knee injury, lower back pain, ACL risk | Add RDLs, hip thrusts, Nordic curls |
| Weak glutes with strong hip flexors | Lower back pain, hip impingement | Add glute activation drills; hip flexor stretching |

## PART 4 — OVERTRAINING AND OVERREACHING

### Signs of Functional Overreaching (Short-Term — Recoverable)

- Reduced performance (can't hit usual weights/reps) for 1–2 weeks
- Increased perceived effort at same workload
- Muscle soreness lasting > 5 days
- Mild mood decline; motivation drop

**Fix:** 5–7 days reduced volume (deload); prioritize sleep and nutrition.

---

### Signs of Non-Functional Overreaching / Overtraining Syndrome (Serious)

| Symptom | Description |
|---|---|
| Performance decline > 2 weeks despite rest | Central nervous system fatigue |
| Resting heart rate elevated by 5–10 bpm consistently | Sympathetic overdrive |
| Persistent fatigue not relieved by sleep | Systemic inflammation |
| Frequent illness / infections | Immune suppression |
| Sleep disturbance (insomnia or excessive sleep) | Hormonal dysregulation |
| Mood disturbances (irritability, depression, anxiety) | HPA axis disruption |
| Loss of appetite | Metabolic disturbance |

**Fix:** 2–4 weeks full rest or very light activity; nutrition audit; sleep optimization. Consult a doctor if symptoms persist > 4 weeks.

> Source: NSCA; ACSM; Sports Medicine literature

---

### Overtraining Risk Triggers (Flag in App)

| Pattern | Flag Level |
|---|---|
| 6+ high-intensity sessions in one week | Yellow — suggest monitoring |
| 0 rest days in 7-day window | Red — flag immediately |
| Same muscle group trained 3+ days in a row | Red — injury risk |
| DOMS still present when same muscle is logged again | Yellow — suggest delaying session |
| Performance declining 3+ sessions in a row | Yellow — suggest deload |
| > 8 weeks without a deload week | Yellow — proactively suggest deload |

## PART 5 — RED FLAGS: STOP TRAINING AND SEEK HELP

These symptoms during or after exercise require stopping and seeking medical attention:

| Symptom | Urgency | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Chest pain or tightness during exercise | 🔴 Emergency | Stop immediately; call emergency services |
| Shortness of breath disproportionate to effort | 🔴 Emergency | Stop; assess; seek medical care |
| Dizziness, lightheadedness, or fainting | 🔴 Emergency | Stop; sit/lie down; seek care |
| Sharp, sudden pain in a joint during lifting | 🔴 Stop session | Rest, ice, elevation; see physiotherapist |
| "Pop" or "snap" sound in a joint or muscle | 🔴 Stop session | Suspected tear; immediate rest; seek medical evaluation |
| Joint swelling after exercise | 🟠 Next-day care | Rest; see GP/physio |
| Pain that persists > 72h after exercise | 🟠 Next-day care | Consult physiotherapist |
| Numbness or tingling in limbs | 🟠 Next-day care | Could indicate nerve issue; see doctor |
| Swollen, hot, red joint | 🟠 Next-day care | Possible infection or inflammatory condition |
| Pain that worsens during warm-up (not improves) | 🟡 Flag | Modify or skip exercise; not typical DOMS |

## PART 6 — COMMON EXERCISE-SPECIFIC OVERUSE INJURIES

| Injury | Exercise Trigger | Prevention |
|---|---|---|
| **Shoulder Impingement** | Overhead press, bench press, pull-ups | Face pulls; rear delt work; reduce overhead volume; improve thoracic mobility |
| **Rotator Cuff Tendinitis** | Any pressing or overhead movement | Band external rotations; Y-T-W raises; 1:2 push:pull ratio |
| **Patellar Tendinitis ("Jumper's Knee")** | Squats, lunges, box jumps | Reduce volume; eccentric squats; quad-to-hamstring balance |
| **IT Band Syndrome** | Running, cycling | Strengthen glutes + hip abductors; reduce mileage; foam roll |
| **Lower Back Strain** | Deadlift, squat, bent-over row | Neutral spine; brace core; reduce load; strengthen erectors + abs |
| **Bicep Tendinopathy** | Heavy curls, pull-ups, rows | Reduce curling volume; eccentric curls; address shoulder position |
| **Tennis Elbow (Lateral Epicondylitis)** | Rows, forearm work, gripping | Reduce gripping volume; forearm stretching; eccentric wrist exercises |
| **Wrist Pain** | Front squats, overhead press, push-ups | Wrist wraps; mobility work; check wrist position in exercises |

## PART 7 — WHEN TO REFER TO A PROFESSIONAL

| Situation | Refer To |
|---|---|
| Joint pain lasting > 2 weeks | Physiotherapist / Sports Medicine Doctor |
| Suspected muscle tear (pop + sudden pain + swelling) | Emergency / Orthopedic Surgeon |
| Lower back pain radiating to legs | Doctor / Neurologist (rule out disc herniation) |
| Shoulder instability or dislocation history | Orthopedic Surgeon / Physio |
| Overtraining syndrome symptoms > 4 weeks | Sports Medicine Doctor |
| Beginner with significant BMI or cardiovascular risk | GP / Cardiologist pre-clearance |
| Pain that prevents normal daily activities | GP / Physiotherapist |
| Persistent knee swelling | Orthopedic evaluation |
